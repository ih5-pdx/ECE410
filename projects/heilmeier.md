### Heilmeier Catechism 

### 1. What are you trying to do?
The goal of this project is to design and evaluate a small hardware accelerator that speeds up matrix multiplication, which is one of the core computations used in modern artificial intelligence and machine learning systems. Instead of performing this work only in software on a general-purpose processor, the project will build a dedicated hardware unit that focuses on multiply-accumulate operations and executes them more efficiently. This accelerator will serve as a proof of concept for how specialized hardware can improve the performance of AI workloads.

To make the project concrete and measurable, the hardware will be evaluated using workloads motivated by neural network inference, especially the convolution and linear algebra patterns seen in models such as ResNet-18. Since the profiling results show that the most computationally expensive parts of the network are dominated by repeated multiply-and-accumulate operations, matrix multiplication provides a meaningful starting point for acceleration. The project will include designing the architecture, verifying correct operation, interfacing it with a host system, and comparing its speed and efficiency against a software-only baseline.

The final objective is not to build a full commercial AI chip, but to demonstrate that even a small custom hardware block can provide measurable benefit on a structured AI-related kernel. Success will be shown through correct numerical results, successful hardware-software integration, and benchmark data demonstrating improvement over a CPU-based implementation.

### 2. How is it done today, and what are the limits of current practice?
Today, matrix multiplication is typically executed in software on CPUs or accelerated using GPUs and other large parallel processors. CPUs are flexible and easy to program, but they are designed for many different kinds of tasks rather than one repeated operation. As a result, they often spend extra time and energy on instruction handling, memory movement, and control overhead rather than just performing the arithmetic itself. For workloads with large numbers of repeated multiply-and-add operations, this limits performance.

GPUs improve on this by providing massive parallelism and are widely used for neural network training and inference. They can deliver very high throughput for matrix-heavy workloads, which is why they dominate many AI applications today. However, GPUs are also large, power-hungry, and architecturally complex. They require substantial software support, memory bandwidth, and programming infrastructure, making them impractical as the target for a small educational hardware design project.

The main limitation of current practice, in the context of this project, is that general-purpose systems are not optimized specifically for a single recurring AI kernel. CPUs are too general, while GPUs are too large and complicated for the intended scope. This leaves room for a smaller, focused accelerator that can target the same mathematical pattern with lower complexity and a clearer connection between the algorithm and the hardware implementation.

### 3. What is new in your approach and why do you think it will be successful?
The new aspect of this project is the decision to isolate a single important AI computation kernel—matrix multiplication—and implement it as a dedicated hardware unit rather than relying entirely on a general-purpose processor. This allows the design to focus on the repeated arithmetic pattern that appears throughout neural network workloads. Instead of building a broad processor that must support many unrelated tasks, the project concentrates hardware resources on the operation that matters most for performance.

This approach is likely to succeed for several reasons. First, matrix multiplication has a highly regular structure, making it well suited for hardware implementation through parallel multiply-accumulate elements, pipelining, and predictable data movement. Second, correctness is straightforward to verify because hardware results can be directly compared with known-good software outputs. Third, the relevance to AI is strong: profiling of networks such as ResNet-18 shows that the heaviest layers are built around repeated multiply-accumulate behavior, meaning the chosen kernel is not arbitrary but directly connected to a real model.

In other words, the project reduces complexity without losing meaning. By targeting a computation that is both central to AI and friendly to hardware acceleration, the design has a strong chance of showing clear performance benefits while remaining manageable within the time and scope of the assignment.
